Accessibility and inclusive design


Targeting accessibility and usability issues with legacy tech, and turning it into an education opportunity

Impact

Backstory

During a quiet period at work, my manager instructed me to investigate one of our legacy services, and identify area's of opportunity for improvements.

This technology was a contact form attached to our public website. It was aimed at user's of our legacy software, and had very low traffic rates, so appetite for improvements was low.

I had no specific brief to work to, simply familiarise myself with the program and develop a proposal of what I would tackle first if given the chance

Planning

Inclusive persona's

To avoid any biases or assumptions from the start, I wanted to map out some key person'a representing our vulnerable customers who may be using the platform.

These persona's were simplistic, but covered a core basis of user types - including physical disabilities, neurodiversity, situational impairements as well as our "assumed" user with no additional needs as a control group for identifying general issues.

Explorers

Explorers want raw data and the power to play with it, expecting strong visuals, customisation opportunities, and the ability to see the different stories data can tell. They're more likely to spend longer on the page and make use of the full tool suite.

Finders

Finders are advisers and their accompanying ParaPlanner staff who will benefit from a new reporting tool but want to use it for immediate fact finding and data exports. Even in a positive experience there goal is getting in and getting out as fast as possible.

Accessibility testing

Visually there were already plenty of findings, but the key focus for a project like this had to be examining the flows - seeing how successful, or unsuccessful, I could be in completing desired actions.

I set out a few basic, common tasks which I could then test with each persona in mind. This allowed me to explore further tools such as mobile responsiveness, screen reader configuration and keyboard navigation.

Not only was this a fantastic way to uncover issues, but it really helped cement my accessibiliity knowledge through gaining some more lived experience using assistive tools and technology

    From the lens of each of my persona's I wanted to discover how easy/difficult it was to:
  • Navigate the available topics
  • Complete an inquiry about nominating beneficiaries
  • Update my personal details

Findings

How to present an accessibility audit?

In my week of exploration, I uncovered around 90 different issues with the portal in my limited scope of testing.

This was far too much to take to the stakeholders in charge of this service. I also had to be mindful I was not dealing with technical stakeholders, but business leaders focused on getting a return on investment into there propositions.

As persona's had been so helpful for me in uncovering issues and creating a story to the metrics, I chose to weave my persona's throughout the presentation.

I was no longer telling them for example that because the page title was wrong it failed WCAG 2.4.2 at A level , but instead weaving a tale about poor Sam who opened a new tab to find there plan number and can no longer spot which of there 10 tabs open contains the form they were completing.

Prioritising fixes

As much as I'd have loved to fix everything, I had to be realistic about what could be pitched to stakeholders. I focused on the most critical functionality issues and A level WCAG failings such as buttons with no focus states or dead links

Long term the hope would be to pick up more cosmetic defects, but in this moment as a designer the far more important call was prioritising usability over aesthetics

When pointing out technical issues, such as buttons lacking focus states or incorrect alternative text, I made sure to also include prescribed fixes, making it easier to hand over my final results

Post-audit activities

The slide deck containing my audit, while originally intended for a small group of immediate stakeholders, gained traction internally as an example of how we should be addressing accessibility concerns and why it matters. To date, I've received over 100 access requests from senior leaders throughout the business to help them understand our issues and empathise with them, including members of the C-suite

I've also shared this presentation with other departments including technology and product management to help them better understand some of the accessibility concerns, and prevent them being repeated in the future

Accessibility toolkit

Inclusive design and accessibility has also become a professional specialty to me, and at our most recent team day, featuring 30 participants from designers, researchers and management, I took the opportunity to run an Inclusive Design workshop.

I designed a hands-on accessibility training workshop to help teams experience barriers different users face, building skills from empathy rather than from textbooks.

This workshop involved splitting into teams and receiving two persona cards, detailing realistic and relatable barriers like language, chronic health or money worries, and a common situation they had to design for.

Insights

  • 90+accessibility and usability issues found in production legacy code
  • 100 access requestsfrom across the organisation for audit findings
  • Learnings transformed into a workshop for 30 UX researchers, designers, managers and senior leadership

What I learned

While a side of desk task, this was a great learning and development opportunity. It proved that there is always more to learn on accessibility and inclusive technology. It also helped me understand some organisational resistance to accessibility enhancements - it's less about not seeing value and more about not fully understanding the scope, impact and benefits of it.
