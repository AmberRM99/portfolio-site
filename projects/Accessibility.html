Accessibility and Inclusive Design

Turning a neglected legacy service into an accessibility learning opportunity.

90+ issues identified 100+ internal requests Workshop for 30 colleagues

I conducted an accessibility audit of a legacy service, using inclusive personas and assistive technology testing to uncover usability barriers. By translating WCAG issues into human stories, I helped stakeholders understand the impact and sparked wider internal conversations about accessibility.

Backstory

During a quiet period at work, my manager instructed me to investigate one of our legacy services, and identify areas of opportunity for improvements.

This technology was a contact form attached to our public website. It was aimed at user's of our legacy software, and had very low traffic rates, so appetite for improvements was low.

I had no specific brief to work to, simply familiarise myself with the program and develop a proposal of what I would tackle first if given the chance

Planning

Generating personas

To avoid any biases or assumptions from the start, I wanted to map out some key person'a representing our vulnerable customers who may be using the platform.

These personas were simplistic, but covered a core basis of user types - including physical disabilities, neurodiversity, situational impairments as well as our "assumed" user with no additional needs as a control group for identifying general issues.

Explorers

Explorers want raw data and the power to play with it, expecting strong visuals, customisation opportunities, and the ability to see the different stories data can tell. They're more likely to spend longer on the page and make use of the full tool suite.

Finders

Inclusive personas

Assumed Sam

Sam has no perceived accessibility needs, and accesses the platform from her home computer, equipped with a keyboard, mouse and high-speed reliable internet connection.

Understanding our assumed user

Our assumed user is thought to face no limitations or handicaps to how they access our products. They are thought to be in good health, neurotypical, able-bodied, and have access to a good-quality computer and high- speed internet. We also assume they have set aside time to complete their tasks and will not face any external disturbances. These assumptions allow them the use of a mouse and keyboard

Key needs
  • An intuitive, well designed user experience Logical instructions and forms to complete desired actions quickly and efficiently
  • Signposting to any further actions required

Visually Impaired Sam

Sam has poorly managed diabetes which has caused diabetic retinopathy and is now registered legally blind. He uses a screen reader to help him get about the web.

Understanding visual impaired users

Visual impaired users are individuals who have difficulty seeing or are completely blind. There is more to vision impairment than the amount visible, users may have a form of colour blindness, lose their glasses, or be in an environment with harsh lighting conditions causing screen glare. Depending on the severity of the impairment, various assistive technologies can be employed such as screen readers, magnifiers, and braille displays.

Key needs
  • Scalable content that works with magnification
  • Alternative text to audibly capture the on screen experience
  • Access to skip links to quickly complete web actions

Motor Impaired Sam

Sam was recently diagnosed with Parkinson's disease after noticing persistent tremors, especially in her hands. She has been trialling navigating the web using her keyboard as she finds a mouse too tricky to use precisely.

Understanding motor impaired users

Users can have a varying degree of limited mobility, which may cause issues typing, scrolling, clicking buttons, or using a mouse. Motor impairments can stem from chronic conditions or disability such as paralysis or tremor disorders, as well as temporary restrictions such as a fractured wrist or multitasking. Assistive technologies allowing alternative inputs, such as speech recognition software, specialized keyboards, or eye- tracking devices, can be used.

Key needs
  • Visible, obvious focus states highlighting where user is within page
  • Forgiveness and ability to correct mis-clicks
  • Interactive content must accept input from mouse alternatives including keyboard navigation and voice inputs

Neurodiverse Sam

Sam is autistic, and while it generally doesn't limit them, they find tasks with vague instructions infuriating and like to have all the information possible.

Understanding neurodiverse users

Neurodivergent web users are individuals with neurological differences that affect their ability to process information and interact with the world around them. These users may have conditions such as autism, ADHD, dyslexia, or other cognitive differences, and there is a large variety of support needs within conditions. They may have difficulty processing information quickly, understanding complex language, or focusing for long periods of time.

Key needs
  • Clear, easy to understand instructions
  • Simple interface with well labelled CTAs and minimal clutter
  • Support on how to independently complete tasks and correct any errors that occur

Mobile Phone Sam

Sam spilled water on his computer a few months ago and hasn't been able to afford a replacement, so has been getting by using just his phone.

Understanding mobile phone users

Using a mobile phone is not by any means an impairment. However, users accessing us via mobile devices require additional considerations, such as how design scales and how much content is visible at a time. Using the web on a mobile device opens up new ways to interact with the content, thinking about scrolling, pinching and tapping. Depending on the environment they're using their phone in, they may also fall temporarily under other accessibility categories.

Key needs

  • Content needs to be designed with responsiveness in mind to suit smaller screen
  • Interactive content needs to be scaled so it can be clicked by clumsy fingers

Findings

How to present an accessibility audit?

In my week of exploration, I uncovered around 90 different issues with the portal.

Issue findings varied drastically; from buttons with no visible focus state, page title's simply named container, all the way through to misleading language where even I, an employee of the company, couldn't work out what we were asking the user for.

This was far too much to take to the stakeholders in charge of this service. I also had to be mindful I was not dealing with technical stakeholders, but business leaders focused on return on investment into their propositions.

As personas had been so helpful for me in uncovering issues and creating a story to the metrics, I chose to weave my personas throughout the presentation.

I was no longer telling them for example that because the page title was wrong it failed WCAG 2.4.2 at A level , but instead weaving a tale about Sam who opened a new tab to find their plan number and can no longer spot which of there 10 tabs open contains the form they were completing.

I got the biggest impact when presenting as I screen-recorded a demo of a page of the current experience, which included over 1 minute of the screen-reader announcing the existing alternative text of an asterisk denoting a mandatory field.

Post-audit activities

The slide deck containing my audit, while originally intended for a small group of immediate stakeholders, gained traction internally as an example of how we should be addressing accessibility concerns and why it matters. To date, I've received over 100 access requests from senior leaders throughout the business to help them understand our issues and empathise with them, including members of the C-suite

I've also shared this presentation with other departments including technology and product management to help them better understand some of the accessibility concerns, and prevent them being repeated in the future

Accessibility toolkit

Inclusive design and accessibility has also become a professional specialty to me, and at our most recent team day, featuring 30 participants from designers, researchers and management, I took the opportunity to run an Inclusive Design workshop.

I designed a hands-on accessibility training workshop to help teams experience barriers different users face, building skills from empathy rather than from textbooks.

This workshop involved splitting into teams and receiving two persona cards, detailing realistic and relatable barriers like language, chronic health or money worries, and a common situation they had to design for.


Insights

  • 100 access requestsfrom across the organisation for my audit findings
  • Learnings transformed into a workshop for 30 UX researchers, designers, managers and senior leadership

What I learned

While a side of desk task, this was a great learning and development opportunity. It proved that there is always more to learn on accessibility and inclusive technology. It also helped me understand some organisational resistance to accessibility enhancements - it's less about not seeing value and more about not fully understanding the scope, impact and benefits of it.
